\begin{abstract}
Kaladont is a word chain game originating in the former Yugoslav region, in which each successive word must begin with the last two letters of the previous one. We present a computational study of how chain playability varies across 25 languages drawn from eight Indo-European branches and one Kartvelian language. Lemmatised word lists are constructed from the English Wiktionary via kaikki.org, filtered through a multi-stage pipeline that removes inflected forms, abbreviations, proper nouns, and — for well-covered languages — low-frequency vocabulary using Zipf score thresholds from the \texttt{wordfreq} library. For each language, 100 games are simulated by random traversal of the word-chain graph. Mean chain lengths range from 2.0 words (Greek) to 40.4 words (Serbo-Croatian, Latin script), with Georgian (34.9) and Serbo-Croatian in Cyrillic (29.6) also ranking highly. South Slavic languages consistently outperform other families; Germanic languages cluster around means of 4–5 words. Lexicon size does not predict chain length reliably, suggesting that the distributional structure of word endings is the primary determinant of playability.
\end{abstract}
