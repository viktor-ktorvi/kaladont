\section{Conclusion}

We simulated Kaladont across 25 languages and found that how well the game plays varies dramatically. Serbo-Croatian and Georgian produced the longest chains (averages of 40.4 and 34.9 words), confirming the intuition from the introduction, while Greek and Hindi produced the shortest (averages of 2.0 and 2.4). Language family is a rough guide but not a reliable one: South Slavic languages consistently rank high, Germanic languages cluster in the middle, but the Baltic languages — Lithuanian and Latvian — diverge sharply despite being closely related.

\subsection{Limitations}

A few caveats are worth keeping in mind. All word lists come from the English Wiktionary, which covers some languages much better than others — smaller or less internationally prominent languages have fewer entries, which may skew the results. The word lists are also imperfect: unusual loanwords, archaic terms, and some inflected forms can slip through the filters. The simulation does not account for the fact that real players only know a fraction of the dictionary, and it does not model strategy — in a real game, a smart player would try to end their word on a two-letter combination that is hard to continue, whereas here the next word is always chosen at random.

\subsection{Future Work}

There are several natural next steps: using Wiktionary editions in the target language (rather than the English edition) for better coverage of smaller languages; simulating strategic play where each player picks the word whose ending is hardest to continue; studying the structure of the word-chain graph itself — which two-letter combinations act as hubs, and how strongly connected the graph is; and extending the study to non-Indo-European language families.
